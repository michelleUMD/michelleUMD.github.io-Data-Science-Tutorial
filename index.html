Tutorial on the Data Science Pipeline¶

Author: Michelle Fang

This tutorial will walk you through the Data Science Pipeline. Here are the following steps of the pipeline:

  1. Data collection/curation
  2. Data management/representation
  3. Exploratory data analysis
  4. Hypothesis testing and machine learning
  5. Communication of insights attained

According to the CDC, breast cancer is the second most common cancer for women in the U.S. The American Cancer Society predicts that in 2021, about 43,600 women will die from breast cancer. It is therefore worthwhile to explore this topic more.

For this tutoral, we will be working with the Haberman's Survival Data Set in order to study survival status in breast cancer patients. This dataset contains information on patients who had undergone breast cancer surgery at the University of Chicago's Billings Hospital between 1958 - 1970. We will try to use variables in this dataset to predict survival status in patients. For more information about the dataset, see the UCI website here.

Before anything else, we will start off by importing various modules that will be useful for our subsequent analyses

Step 1: Data Collection/Curation¶

In this step of the data science pipeline, we will obtain the data and read it into our script. The dataset can be downloaded here.

The dataset is in a comma-separated value format, so we will use a function from pandas to import it. From the UCI website, we can also see that the data has the following attributes, which we can use to label the colummns:

  1. Age of patient at time of operation (numerical)
  2. Patient's year of operation (year - 1900, numerical)
  3. Number of positive axillary nodes detected (numerical)
  4. Survival status (class attribute)
    • 1 = the patient survived 5 years or longer
    • 2 = the patient died within 5 year

Step 4: Hypothesis testing and machine learning¶

Now, let's use some statistics to show quantitative evidence on whether some of the above relationships exist.

Is there a statistically significant difference in mean age between the two survival groups?¶

Here are our hypotheses:

  • $H_0:$ the mean age of the two survival groups are equal

  • $H_a:$ the mean age of the two survival groups are not equal

To test these hypotheses, we could use use a two-sample t-test. However, there are several assumptions that must be met for us to apply the t-test. We will asssume the samples are independent. We also have to check for normality. We did a visual check of this earlier in the exploration phase with the violin plots and it looked like there was a possibility of a skewed distribution. Let's do a Shapiro-Wilk test to check if the distributions are normal.

In [144]:
print(stats.shapiro(np.array(ages_for_surv_group['age'][0])))
print(stats.shapiro(np.array(ages_for_surv_group['age'][1])))

ShapiroResult(statistic=0.9842116236686707, pvalue=0.013179867528378963)
ShapiroResult(statistic=0.9763393402099609, pvalue=0.13881994783878326)

One of the distributions had a p-value of < 0.05. This means this sample is not normal, so we cannot use a parametric statistical test.

Instead, let's use the non-parametric Wilcoxon-Mann-Whitney test that does not assume normality.

In [145]:
stats.mannwhitneyu(ages_for_surv_group['age'][0], ages_for_surv_group['age'][1])
Out[145]:
MannwhitneyuResult(statistic=8446.0, pvalue=0.16459031990274325)

We got a p value of > 0.05. This means that while the mean age for those that survived at least 5 years was slightly lower than those who did not survive past 5 years, this difference is not statistically significant. We cannot reject our null hypothesis that the true population mean ages of the two types of survivors is different.

Are number of positive lymph nodes associated with survival status?¶

Here, our null hypothesis is there is no association between number of positive nodes and survival status. The alternative hypothesis is there is an association.

To test these hypotheses, we will use a logistic regression model. You can read more about logistic regression here.

Here, we will use machine learning to fit this model. The dataset we have will serve as a training set, and the algorithm will attempt to fit the model based on that.

Cross-validation

Fitting the logistic regression model to the entire dataset does not tell us much about how the model would do for data it has never seen before. Because of this, it is useful to do cross-validation. In this case, we only train the algorithm on a subset of the dataset, and use the rest of the dataset to test out how the model did. Here, we will do 10-fold cross-validation, in which we split the dataset into 10 subsets, comparing one subset to the remaining 9 each time.

In [148]:
#Loop over each fold
kf = KFold(n_splits=10)

#make list to store coefficients and accuracies 
coefficients = list()
accuracies = list()

for train_index, test_index in kf.split(ind):
    
    #Split up datset into training and test data
    X_train, X_test = np.array(ind)[train_index], np.array(ind)[test_index]
    y_train, y_test = [dep[i] for i in train_index], [dep[i] for i in test_index]
    
    
    ######## Fit decision tree ########
    reg = LogisticRegression().fit(X_train, y_train)
    
    #See how we did
    predicted = reg.predict(X_test)
    actual = np.array(y_test)
    predictions = np.array(predicted)
    
    #Add coefficients and accuracy scores to the list 
    coefficients.append(reg.coef_)
    accuracies.append(met.accuracy_score(actual, predictions))
    
    #Print out results
    print("Coefficient: ", reg.coef_)
    print("Confusion Matrix: \n", met.confusion_matrix(actual, predictions))
    print("Accuracy: ", met.accuracy_score(actual, predictions), "\n")

Step 5: Communication of insights attained¶

Overall, we learned:

  • There does not appear to be a trend when it comes to percentage of breast cancer surgery patients survivng at least 5 years over time
  • We do not have evidence to conclude there is a difference in mean age between patients who survived less than 5 years and those who survived at least 5 years
  • We have evidence to believe that number of positive axillary nodes is associated with survival status
    • The odds ratio (95% confidence interval) for when number of nodes is treated as a numerical variable is 1.09 (1.05, 1.13)
    • The odds ratio (95% confidence interval) for when number of nodes is treated as a categorical variable is 3.53 (1.99, 6.29)

The implication of the first finding is that there does not appear to be any major trends in survival rate between 1958 and 1962 at the University of Chicago's Billings Hospital. The second finding tells us age is not a very strong predictor for if a patient will survive at least 5 years after surgery.

The final finding gives us insight about predicting prognosis of the cancer. Patients who have positive lymph nodes are less likely to survive at least 5 years, with a ~253% increased chance of dying within the first 5 years. More specifically, each additional positive lymph node increases the risk of dying within 5 years by ~9%.
